The sites are hiding
in plain sight.

Within a short walk of Fresh Pond and Alewife station sit acres of single-story rooftops and surface parking — land that could carry thousands of mixed-use homes without demolishing anyone's house or felling a street's canopy. Here is where thoughtful density belongs, and what it should look like.

Why the market alone won't build it

Today's development pattern is capital-led: projects happen where and when the return clears the bar, and the surest return is the highest-margin product — small luxury units. Left alone, that process is slow, opportunistic, and selective. It optimizes each parcel's profit, not the city's needs — nobody in the pipeline is responsible for the whole: where family-sized homes come from, where the affordable half lives, where the grocery store and the community room go.

That is not an argument against developers — it is an argument for a city that sets the program and invites them to build it. Cambridge did exactly that with the Affordable Housing Overlay. The same clarity, applied to the sites below, gets the homes without the harm.

Site 01 · Fresh Pond

Above Whole Foods — Fresh Pond Mall

Alewife Brook Parkway at New Street

A one-story supermarket and big-box strip wrapped in surface parking, directly across the parkway from Fresh Pond Reservation. The grocery store is the anchor a neighborhood needs — so keep it, and build homes over it: a podium of shops and services with six to eight stories of mixed-income apartments above, courtyards facing the pond-side light.

Site 02 · Fresh Pond

Above Trader Joe's — Fresh Pond Shopping Center

Alewife Brook Parkway retail strip

The same pattern one block on: Trader Joe's, a strip of single-story retail, and a sea of asphalt. This is the most car-oriented corner of a city that says it wants transit-oriented living. Build over and around the stores — ground floors that stay retail and restaurants, homes above, the parking consolidated below grade or in a wrapped structure.

Site 03 · Alewife

Alewife Station — the connectivity site

Red Line terminus · Minuteman Bikeway · Route 2

A subway terminus, the region's flagship bike path, and a highway gateway — wrapped in a five-story parking garage and buffered by the Alewife Brook Reservation's existing park landscape, with Jerry's Pond beside it. Almost nowhere in Greater Boston offers this much connectivity next to this much underused hardscape. Rebuild the garage block as a true station neighborhood: homes over transit, a public square at the headhouse, the reservation as its front yard.

Site 04 · Alewife

CambridgePark Drive lots

Between the station and the quadrangle

Office-park surface lots a few hundred feet from the platform — the textbook definition of underused transit-adjacent land. Infill the parking, keep the offices, add the ground-floor restaurants and services the area conspicuously lacks, and connect it all to the station and the bikeway with real streets instead of driveways.

Site 05 · Alewife

The Quadrangle — do the master plan right

Smith Place · Fawcett Street · Concord Avenue

Low-rise industrial and commercial blocks where a ~4 million sq ft master plan (the Healthpeak PUD, PB-410) is already before the Planning Board. The site is right; the question is the program. Measured against our tests: how much of it is homes rather than labs, how many of those homes are affordable and family-sized, does height step to city scale, and does the flood-prone quadrangle get the parks and drainage it owes the 2070 map?
